Jennifer Anne Muirhead

Student No. s3893768

My name is Jennifer, I’m Scottish-Australian, and I like solving puzzles. I have an odd amount of formal qualifications for an early twenty-something (hint: more than 5) and am beginning the trend anew with online courses. I got my start in programming with C through Harvard’s CS50 and am currently enrolled in RMIT studying Java (though edX and I still flirt occasionally). Never a language monogamist, I also speak a wee bit of Korean and Polish. Most importantly, I have a cat whose name is Chonky.

Test One

Firstly, looking at the Myers-Briggs’ test, some of my positive characteristics are that I am, or at least can be, rational, informed, independent, determined, curious and versatile. I tend to seek out information, try to expand my knowledge, base my opinions on facts, as opposed to feelings, don’t rely on others, work hard to achieve my goals, explore new ideas, and am willing to try many things in order to do so.

For obvious reasons, these traits can be helpful when trying to complete a task, and as such have proved helpful in a work or school based setting. That is, of course, until they go unchecked and become the extreme, undesirable versions of themselves. Confidence becomes arrogance, independence becomes the inability to work with others, and flexiblity becomes lability. Because I tend to be evidence-based, if I find myself in a position where I am asked to do something without a good explanation of why, I can come off as combative and questioning of authority.

Test Two

I didn’t find the VARK questionnaire particularly useful because my immediate thought with almost all questions was “well, it depends”. Then when I read the options, I selected all of them, most of the time. The questionnaire really struck me as a “would you like more information or less information?”, as opposed to “which information would you like?”

For instance, when given the prompt: “When learning from the Internet I like:
  • videos showing how to do or make things.
  • audio channels where I can listen to podcasts or interviews.
  • interesting written descriptions, lists and explanations.
  • interesting design and visual features”
All I could think of is “well, it depends, but ‘yes’”. I also noticed that a lot of the questions were targeted at beginners, or people doing things forthe first time. I think a beginner, an intermediate, and an expert, would benefit from different types of explanations based on their experience level more so than their learning style. And this is without even mentioning that different topics can be explained better/easier with certain styles.

Test Three

My Big Five personality score revealed that I’m slightly more introverted than I am extroverted, which I would agree with. Not necessarily because I don’t like socialising or that I’m not good at it, but I find it can be quite draining and it’s not where I get my energy from. I tend to socialise in small bouts, then go off on my own to recharge.

It also revealed that I’m a reasonably emotionally stable person who can handle stress and is slow to anger and other negative emotions.

The test believes I’m slightly more agreeable than disagreeable, which I would agree with in everyday life - I’m more than happy to go with what the other person wants for things like where we should eat, or what we should watch (things I consider mostly unimportant) - but not when it comes to important things, like work or assessments. In those cases, I think we should do what will result in the most desirable outcome, or most promising idea. Which, I can acknowledge, is not always mine, though I won’t easily acquiesce if I think mine is better.

I am a highly conscientious person who’s disciplined and well organised. I tend to implement structures, timelines, to-do lists, and other time and progress trackers to keep on top of my tasks. I like to produce good work on time.

I scored roughly 50/50 on intellect/Imagination, previously known as openness to experience which surprised me slightly. Though, if considered from the perspective that I neither seek out the traditional/the expected/the usual, or the new/the untested/the unusual, I would agree. I don’t inherently go for either extreme, despite being open to both of them. It depends entirely on the context and specific scenario.

I have not written a lot about these tests because I honestly do not think too much of them. I do not believe the results mean anything useful. You cannot develop a critical opinion about someone based upon a generic online test.

Furthermore, I would never consider these tests to find "appropriate team members" as so many others do while gathering people for a project. If you really want to know somebody's personality you should interact with that person for yourself and form your own opinion.

The creativity test says that I am slightly below average when it comes to creative thinking. However, it would not be wise for me to limit my involvement in creative team tasks. The same logic applies for a high creativity score i.e. I should not jump in and take over a creative team task because "I'm better according to a test I found on the internet".

The point is, do not sell yourself short because a test says you are not good enough. But do not get a big head either if the test goes in your favour. I personally would much rather work with someone who is open minded and is prepared to consider the thoughts, ideas, and opinions of others even if their ideas make more sense.

Myers-Briggs Test

I have recently undertaken a number of tests that may be used by employers during a recruitment process. The tests provided certain insights into my personality and learning style. A brief summary of the results of each test is detailed below. Myers-Briggs Test After completing the Myers Briggs test, I got ESFJ-A or “Consul”(16 Personalities, 2011-2021). The Myers Briggs results were very detailed and included information about how my personality type’s strengths and weaknesses and approach to relationships and my career. Broadly, the test results reflect that Consuls are popular people, who enjoy being in the spotlight and leading others. People with a Consul personality take responsibility seriously and will base their moral compass on established traditions and law. Consuls enjoy participating in discussion, respect hierarchy and are loyal partners and employees. I feel that the result of Consul is an accurate representation of my personality. I do enjoy being of service and consider myself supportive and outgoing. I have the personality type to be a good leader and I am good at managing several projects at one time. The results provide a detailed list of strengths and weaknesses, which I believe will influence my behaviour in a team environment. These weaknesses obviously have the capacity to adversely impact those around me and have a negative influence on my team. In terms of how this will influence forming a team, I believe it is important to assemble a team of people who complement each other. Where I have an identified weakness, it is important to have others within a team that have a strength in that area.

My Learning Style

In addition to the Myer’s Briggs test, I also took a test to assess my learning style (EducationPlanner, 2020). The results reflect that I am 40% auditory, 40% visual and 20% tactile, meaning that I am an Auditory/Visual Learner and learn through a combination of listening and looking. The test provided several tips to assist with auditory learning, including use of flashcards, reading information out loud and recording myself. As I am also a visual learner, the test recommended visualising things as I hear them, writing down key instructions and drawing pictures to explain new concepts. My learning style requires both audio and visual prompts. Now that I am aware of this, I can ensure that I utilise the recommendations to ensure I am absorbing information as best I can. Through ensuring I am implementing best practice in my studies, I can ensure I am making the best possible contribution to my team. This will also influence how I form a team as I consider it would be beneficial if all team members had similar learning styles.

Integrity Test

I completed this test as I believe that honesty and integrity are qualities that all employers will think highly of. I received an honesty outlook of 78/100. For me, I think that this is a good result and reflects that I am trustworthy. I think that integrity is an important part of any team because you have to be able to trust your teammates. The test is indicative of the fact that people who are inclined to time theft and cyberloafing may be more inclined to take part in dishonest behaviours. If I observed these behaviours in a person, I would be less likely to consider including that person when forming a team.
