Autonomous Vehicles

Glenn Shi

What does it do?

An autonomous vehicle is a vehicle than can sense its environment and is able to operate without human involvement. The vehicle does not require a human passenger to take control, nor does it need a human passenger to be inside the vehicle at all. An autonomous vehicle can do everything and go anywhere that an experienced human driver and a traditional car can. (What is an Autonomous Car? – How Self-Driving Cars Work | Synopsys, 2021)

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) currently defines 6 levels of driving automation ranging from Level 0 (fully manual) to level 5 (fully autonomous). These have now been globally accepted as the standard for autonomous vehicle development.

  • Level 0: No autonomous functionality whatsoever, a traditional automobile operated exclusively by a human.
  • Level 1: Most driving functions are handled by a human, but one specific function (such as accelerating and decelerating with an adaptive cruise control system) is handled by the automobile.
  • Level 2: The automobile assumes all basic tasks (acceleration, braking, steering) associated with driving under some circumstances, but human intervention is required if the system miscalculates, and the human driver must always monitor the vehicle and its surroundings and be prepared to retake control.
  • Level 3: The automobile assumes all basic tasks associated with driving and monitors the vehicle and its environment to respond to unplanned circumstances. Human involvement is theoretically only required in case of an emergency scenario in which the vehicle fails to respond properly to its environment.
  • Level 4: The automobile assumes all driving tasks in specified environments (such as traffic jams), but a human driver is still required, and the vehicle must be capable of parking itself safely if the human driver is unable to retake control.
  • Level 5: Full automation with no human input required, the vehicle operates under its own control and can travel anywhere at any time without human occupants.
(Semi-Autonomous Vehicles: Avoidable Risk and The Essence of Fault, 2021)

Autonomous cars rely on a wide array of sensors, actuators, complex algorithms, machine learning systems, and powerful processors to execute software. They can create and maintain a map of their surroundings based on a variety of sensors situated in different parts of the vehicle. Radar sensors can monitor the position of nearby vehicles. Video cameras can detect traffic lights, read road signs, track other vehicles, and look for pedestrians. Lidar (light detection and ranging) sensors can bounce pulses of light off the car’s surroundings to measure distances, detect road edges, and identify lane markings. Ultrasonic sensors in the wheels can detect curbs and other vehicles when parking.

Innovative software can then process all these sensory inputs, plot a path, and send instructions to the car’s actuators, which control acceleration, braking, and steering. Hard-coded rules, obstacle avoidance algorithms, predictive modelling, and object recognition help the software follow traffic rules and navigate obstacles. (What is an Autonomous Car? – How Self-Driving Cars Work | Synopsys, 2021)

What is the likely impact?

The advancement to a fully automated vehicle will not be a new or sudden concept, but more of a continuing process with plenty of trials and tests, accumulating crucial information that is still lacking. They will launch in different phases, varying with the level of autonomy. A level 5 fully autonomous vehicle will probably take another decade or so before they hit the roads. However, level 4 autonomous cars will likely be seen moving on the roads within the next few years. (The Future of Autonomous Cars | May 2020, 2021).

The scenarios for convenience and quality of life improvements with the development of autonomous vehicles are almost limitless. You could send your children to school using the car, and it could return home. The elderly and the physically disabled would have the freedom and independence to go wherever they wanted. It could drop you off at a point and head to a detected vacant parking spot rather than you having to drive around looking for one. If someone perhaps forgot something at home, you could even send it to them using the car. (What is an Autonomous Car? – How Self-Driving Cars Work | Synopsys, 2021).

Perhaps the most important benefit of autonomous vehicles would be the decrease in number of accidents.They can prevent human errors from happening as the system controls the vehicle. It leaves no opportunity for distraction, unlike humans who are prone to interruptions. It also uses complicated algorithms that determine the correct stopping distance between one vehicle to another. As autonomous vehicles replace manual vehicles on the road, inevitably this will remove the roles and responsibilities of humans in driving. This could impact the employment rate of services such as taxi drivers, truck drivers and pilots, as is similar for many other industries that face automation. (Advantages And Disadvantages Of Autonomous Car – Half Fast Chicago, 2021)

Autonomous vehicles could result in a significant reduction in traffic congestion and faster travel times. Autonomous cars could communicate with each other, as well as complex automated highway systems which could direct traffic and identify traffic problems early. As there are fewer accidents and less traffic congestion, there would be a significant drop in harmful emissions. According to Ohio University’s Future of Driving report, harmful emissions will be reduced by 60% after the advent of autonomous vehicles. (7 benefits of autonomous cars, 2021)

How will this affect you?

Currently I have a Learners driving license, and I am progressing towards a full driving license. As I do not drive, I primarily use public transport such as the train as my main mode of transport, otherwise I would be a passenger in a car, with someone else driving. Although I do plan to drive myself sooner or later, autonomous vehicles would mostly likely become my primary mode of transport once it completes development. In the most likely scenario, autonomous vehicles will replace almost all manual vehicles, such as autonomous trains and buses. So even though I may still use public transport it would likely still be autonomous.

I would probably have my own personal autonomous vehicle, and the same would be for my family and friends. I would have a faster journey, and therefore save time that would have been spent travelling. Even in the vehicle, as I am no longer required to manually drive the car, I can spend the time during the journey for my own personal use, such as watching a film or doing some work.

Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity

Thomas Bradford

Today, every facet of our lives is governed by technology. For example, students now use technology almost exclusively to complete their schoolwork, a significant number of workplaces have transitioned to some form of work from home model and in the wake of the global pandemic, we now “check in” at most locations we attend. The result of that is that our personal and sensitive information is not only stored on our personal devices but is also stored on servers around the world due to cloud-based technology.

This kind of technological advancement has changed the way we live our lives. While it has many positive impacts, the transition to primarily online processes has resulted in a heightened threat to individuals and businesses’ information. Specifically, all this information is at significant risk of cyber threats, including the threat of:

  • Cybercrime, which refers to single actors or groups targeting systems for financial gain or to cause disruption;
  • Cyber-attack, which generally involves politically motivated information gathering measures; and
  • Cyberterrorism, which is intended to undermine electronic systems to cause panic or fear in society (Kaspersky Lab, 2021).
The primary threat to everyday people and businesses is the threat of cybercrime. For instance, the threat of malware or “malicious software”, which is software developed by cybercriminals designed to cause damage to a server, computer, or network. A common example of malware is a “virus”, which is a program designed to attach itself to a friendly file. Once the file is on a computer, the virus operates to infect other files on the computer with malicious code. Another example of malware is a “Trojan” which is disguised as normal, legitimate software. Once uploaded onto a computer, a Trojan operates to cause damage and/or collect a user’s data. A further example is “phishing”, which is where a cybercriminal targets a victim via email. The email is designed to appear like it comes from a legitimate source with the intention being to trick the user into replying to the email with their sensitive information, for example, credit card details.

To combat the risk of malware, phishing, and other cyber-attacks, there has been an increased focus on cyber security. Kapersky Lab (2021, para. 1) defines cyber security as “the practice of defending computers, servers, mobile devices, electronic systems, networks, and data from malicious attacks. It's also known as information technology security or electronic information security”. The term applies to a variety of measures, including the protection of network, application, information, and operational security. Together, these facets of cyber security operate to detect and protect everything from personal devices, personal and sensitive information and data assets and computer networks secure from malicious attack. Cyber security operates on an individual level, but also operates to protect businesses and government entities (who hold a significant amount of individual information) from attack.

One of the biggest elements of cybersecurity is anti-malware software which is designed to detect, combat against, and remove things such as malware. Early anti-malware software utilised signature-based strategies to detect known viruses (Malwarebytes, n.d). However, this software was unable to detect new threats. As technology has advanced, anti-malware software has evolved with it. It is now able to detect new threats via the technology of “heuristic analysis”. By using heuristic analysis to analyse program structures and behaviour, these programs are now able to detect unusual code or activity rather than just detecting known viruses.

It is difficult to predict the future of cyber security because of the ever-changing behaviours of cybercriminals and the new attacks these criminals develop. For example, the number of global ransomware hacks increased by nearly 25% from 2018 to 2019, prompting cybersecurity developers and businesses to create innovative software to counter this trend (Parker, 2020).

It is impossible to tell what the next big threat or attack will be, but experts still have a good idea of the direction that we are headed in. Within the next three years, it is expected that Artificial Intelligence (AI) will become both a software vulnerable to attack, as well as a core component of all cyber security systems. AI will likely be embedded into user interfaces to warn users about high-risk websites and programs. Additionally, it may be used to simulate network attacks and identify weaknesses in existing cyber security measures.

We are seeing an increase in technology and the way we use it. The impact of this is that there is a heightened demand for solid and ever evolving cyber security, both for individuals and corporations. It is difficult to predict the impact of cyberattacks and cyber crime in the future. However, it is important as a society, we are alive to the risks of using technology to avoid cyberattacks and malware.

As the risk to information increases alongside our growing reliance on technology, we can expect to see the younger generation being educated on this a lot earlier than previous generations. As we are already starting to see, people will be taught about cybersecurity from a young age. There is not one group of people that will be impacted by the advancement of cybersecurity; this will have a global impact on any person utilising technology in future.

Data is rapidly becoming one of the most valuable assets in today’s society (Tunggal,2021) and the damage that can result from poor cybersecurity measures cannot be understated. It is estimated that in 2020, over 12 billion records were compromised with known ransomware attacks, which represents a rise in attacks of nearly 60 per cent (Karen, 2021). Companies now hold the personal information of millions of people, and there is a growing risk that this information is vulnerable to cyber-attack. For example, in April 2019 almost 540 million records held by Facebook were breached and May 2019, the sensitive information of over 885 million users of the First American Financial Corporation were breached (Tunggal, 2021).

With the increase in such attacks and the corresponding need for cyber security, we are already seeing an expanding demand for IT professionals with a cyber security focus. It is expected that we will continue to see an upsurge in the demand for cyber security jobs within the next decade. This is reflected in the results of a 2017 study, which estimates that, by 2022, the gap between open positions and qualified personnel will widen to 1.8 million jobs (Frost and Sullivan, 2017).

The growth in the need for cyber security experts will directly impact me. This is because it is my intention to one day work in an IT role where cybersecurity knowledge is an essential requirement. It will also impact me in my daily life, as I will need to continue to ensure that I have appropriate software and knowledge to ensure that my personal information remains safe from cyberattack. Similarly, my family and friends will need to ensure that they are aware of the risk of cybercriminals, both in their personal lives and in their employment. This is particularly relevant for people who have transitioned into a work from home environment, where the business information is perhaps more vulnerable than ever.

As the risk to our personal information grows, so too does the need to ensure we have put in place appropriate measures to address such risks. As I am now aware of the various methods that cyber criminals may use to access information, I will be more cognisant in ensuring that my information is protected in the future.

Natural Language Processing and Chatterbots

Natural Language Processing

Jennifer Anne Muirhead

Language comes naturally for most people. A one year old baby may understand as many as fifty words, and be able to make one or two common, simple words like mama or dada (Lam-Cassettari, 2019). Computers have historically struggled with language, at most pretending to have a sort of pseudo understanding in very narrow fields (Pavlus, 2019). Natural Language Processing, NLP, is attempting to improve this. NLP is a branch of Artificial Intelligence that “gives machines the ability to read, understand and derive meaning from human languages” (Xue and Zhang, 2020).

NLP is the driving force behind various applications, many of which are used inconspicuously in day-to-day life. Gmail uses NLP both in front of and behind-the-scenes. The former through spell-check, and the latter through emails being filtered and classified as they “flow through the servers” - stopping spam in the process (Asokan, 2019). A more advanced use of NLP can be seen in the medical field, where it “enables the recognition and prediction of diseases based on electronic health records and a patient's own speech”. Cardiovascular disease, depression, and schizophrenia are all health conditions currently being explored by NLP (Tyag, 2020).

This is possible due to the vast improvements of NLP in recent years, that have come largely as a result of Neural Networks more generally, and Transformers more specifically (Anand, 2020). There are many types of Neural Networks, but generally, they are a form of machine learning, loosely based on the human brain, where a computer learns how to perform a task based on labelled training samples. A Neural Network may have thousands to millions of simple processing nodes, which are often layered and densely interconnected (Hardesty, 2017).

Technological innovations led from a Feedforward model, which only sends data in one direction, to a Recurrent model, where connections between nodes form a directed graph, effectively gaining the ability to retain previously received information in short-term memory.

Although an improvement, RNNs are inherently deficient at retaining information over long periods of time. This is because of something known as the “vanishing gradient” problem in back-propagation (Kobran and Bayns, 2020), illustrated in figure XYZ.

This is an issue in language, as we often re-refer to specific items later in a sentence, such as in the Griezmann example. Subsequently, the next innovation came from Long Short Term Memory, or LSTMs. LSTMs are a type of Recurrent Network able to determine key information, retain it over longer periods of time, and recall the information much later on in the sequence (Kobran and Bayns, 2020).

Part-of-Speech Tagging, PST, is often used with LSTMs to retain information, such as the subject, verb, and object of a sentence. Although tagging doesn’t not solve any particular NLP problem, it is a prerequisite for simplifying and resolving a lot of different, more complex issues (Malhotra and Godayal, 2018). Current accuracy rates for POS tagging are in the 97 per cent (Ruder, n.d.). The downside of PST is that the data used must be labelled - manually tagged - which is time-intensive and causes problems with scalability (Wired, 2016).

These tools have helped to develop and improve the technology for speech recognition, language modeling, and translation, yet the current State of the Art framework is Google’s BERT, which stands for: Bi-Directional Encoder Representations Transformers (Anderson, 2019).

Unlike the others, BERT was bi-directionally trained on unlabeled text. It learned from understanding text cohesion from a large body of content in plain text - more than 2.5 billion words from English Wikipedia - followed by fine-tuning on smaller, more specific natural language tasks (Anderson, 2019).

Currently, BERT is being used at Google to optimize the interpretation of user search queries - a task possible due to BERT’s stellar natural language understanding and sequence-to-sequence based language generation (Nayak, 2019). BERT has advanced the NLP field in numerous ways, including: question answering, abstract summarization, sentence prediction, conversational response generation, polysemy and coreference resolution, word sense disambiguation, natural language inference, and sentiment classification (Lutkevich, 2020).

Which, although impressive, could also be the beginning of the end to many language-dependant jobs, like translation. In 2016, more than 100 billion words were translated each day by Google Translate alone (Turovsky, 2016). In many high-resource languages, like Spanish and Chinese, it has an accuracy rating of around 90%, and these figures have been improving over time (Caswell and Liang, 2020). It is conceivable that with the continued advancements in NLP technology, translation could become an obsolete profession. However, this may not happen for many years to come. Although high-resource languages have high accuracy rates, the inverse is true for low-resource languages like Farsi and Armenian, 67% and 55%, respectively (Wetsman, 2021).

Another promising way in which NLP is being used is in the act of summarisation. Summarisation is becoming an increasingly invaluable asset, as can be seen in the following example published in Fortune: “In 1995, the average daily reading requirement of a U.S. intelligence analyst was 20,000 words. By 2016, the same analyst’s daily reading load was estimated at 200,000 words”. This number, Kahn writes, is more than a human can skim in a day. The phenomenon of insurmountable data is affecting other fields, too, like finance, law, and the sciences. The issue is that accurately summarizing long documents whilst retaining the useful information is difficult for humans, and somewhat subjective. Though there are companies who have developed summarisation tools based on BERT, that can generate reasonably fluent summaries (Kahn, 2020).

Despite, or perhaps because of these advancements, my daily life, just like the life of my friends and family members will likely be affected in many small ways, many of which we likely won’t even notice - a characteristic of good design. For me personally, I might see it in small things like Gmail correcting my misspelling of the word “necessary”, or the company chatterbots I’m fairly sure aren’t human, but I say “please” and “thank you” anyway just in case. But there are some larger ways in which our lives may change, too. Like the disappearing language-barrier, the control over (fake) news (de Oliveira et al., 2021), and whether we get picked for that job or not (Zaroor, Maree and Sabha, 2017).

Natural Language Processing tools like BERT are changing how humans analyze and connect with information. Computers are becoming more capable of comprehending content and performing human tasks. It is not unreasonable to predict that human jobs, especially those in language-dominated fields, will be made easier by these technological improvements, before eventually being replaced by them.
